Simplicity Bias in Neural Networks#

Neural networks (NNs) exhibit a fascinating property often referred to as simplicity bias. As described by Chizat and Bach in their work:

“NNs are fundamentally Bayesian: an NN is biased, at initialization, towards simple functions.”
(Chizat & Bach)

This idea sheds light on why NNs tend to generalize well despite their enormous capacity to memorize complex datasets.#

Understanding Simplicity Bias#

The Setup#

  1. Fix a Neural Network:
    Let $ \Theta$ denote the space of its parameters, where each point $ \theta \in \Theta$ represents a specific configuration of weights and biases of the network.

  2. Functions Represented by the Network:
    The neural network represents a mapping: $$ F_\Theta : \Theta \to \mathcal{F} $$ where $ \mathcal{F}$ is the space of functions that the network can learn.

  3. A Specific Function $F$:
    Any $ F \in \mathcal{F}$ is a specific function the model can realize for a given choice of $\theta$.

  4. Complexity of a Function:
    The complexity $ \mathcal{C}(F)$ measures how “simple” or “complex” the function $F$ is. Formally:
    $$ \mathcal{C}(F) : \mathcal{F} \to [0, +\infty) $$

Linking Parameters to Simplicity#

The simplicity bias emerges from the connection between the parameter space $Theta$ and the complexity of functions $ \mathcal{C}(F) $. Specifically:

  • Probability Distribution on $\Theta $:
    Let $P$ be a probability measure on $ \Theta$, which describes the initialization randomness of the network.

  • Random Sampling of Functions:
    Using $P$, we can randomly pick parameters $ \theta$ and obtain corresponding functions $ F(\theta)$. This makes $ F$ a random variable.

Simplicity Bias as a “Pointwise” Statement#

The simplicity bias can now be formally expressed as: $$ \mathcal{C}(F) \leq \mathcal{C}(G) \implies P(F = f) > P(G = g) $$ This indicates that the neural network is more likely to represent simpler functions under random initialization.
